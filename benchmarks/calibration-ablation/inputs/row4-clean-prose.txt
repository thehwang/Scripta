The Antikythera mechanism is an ancient Greek hand-powered analog computer, discovered in 1901 in the wreck of a Roman-era ship off the small Aegean island of Antikythera. Researchers have dated its construction to between 150 and 100 BC, making it one of the most sophisticated technological artifacts surviving from the ancient world. The device was used to predict astronomical positions and eclipses for calendrical and astrological purposes, and to track the four-year cycle of the ancient Olympic Games.

A particularly remarkable feature is the differential gearing used to calculate the synodic month, the time from one new moon to the next. This is the earliest known use of a mechanical differential in any surviving artifact, and the principle would not appear again in European engineering for more than a thousand years. The intricacy of the gearwork suggests a long tradition of mechanical engineering in the Hellenistic world, although no other mechanism of comparable complexity has been recovered from the period.

Modern study of the device began in earnest in the mid-twentieth century, when X-ray and later micro-CT imaging revealed the internal gear arrangement that had been impossible to see directly through the corroded bronze surface. The most detailed reconstructions, published between 2006 and 2021, have allowed researchers to recover Greek inscriptions on the device's faces describing the operation of its various dials. The mechanism remains the subject of active research, and several reconstructions have been built that demonstrate its predictions match historical records.
